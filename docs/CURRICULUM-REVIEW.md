# Curriculum Review Plan

## Status

This is a product-development curriculum map, not an educator-certified curriculum. Before a wider child beta or App Store launch, the sequence, language, examples, and adaptive rules should be reviewed by an experienced elementary mathematics educator.

## Learning model

Each guided expedition moves through:

1. **Concrete:** counters, baskets, movable groups, and repeated actions.
2. **Pictorial:** arrays, number lines, bar models, fraction pieces, decimal grids, and 10 × 10 pattern grids.
3. **Abstract:** equations, mental strategies, and retrieval without a model.
4. **Application:** a new story or situation using the same relationship.
5. **Explanation:** Teach Purple by selecting or building a valid explanation.
6. **Retention:** return after increasing time intervals.

A skill is not treated as mastered from same-day accuracy alone. Evidence should include recall, visual modeling, application, explanation, and later retrieval.

## Grade 3 priorities

- Addition and subtraction within 1,000
- Place value and flexible mental computation
- Multiplication as equal groups, arrays, repeated jumps, and area
- Division as sharing and grouping
- Relationship between multiplication and division
- One- and two-step word problems
- Unit fractions and fractions on a number line
- Comparing simple fractions with common numerators or denominators
- Measurement, time, area, and perimeter
- Reading and creating simple graphs

## Grade 4 priorities

- Multi-digit addition and subtraction fluency
- Multiplication facts and multiplicative comparison
- Multiplication of larger whole numbers using models and place value
- Division with remainders using models and estimation
- Factors, multiples, and number patterns
- Fraction equivalence, ordering, and addition/subtraction with like denominators
- Decimal notation for tenths and hundredths
- Measurement conversion and multi-step problems
- Angle, geometry, area, and perimeter reasoning

## Review checklist

An educator reviewer should verify:

- Progression from concrete to pictorial to abstract is developmentally appropriate.
- Visuals represent the equation accurately and never imply a false rule.
- Language is understandable to a typical 8–10-year-old.
- Strategy choices are mathematically valid and do not become tricks without meaning.
- Difficulty changes are gradual and reversible.
- A miss triggers support without shame or excessive repetition.
- Skipping a problem schedules support rather than hiding the skill forever.
- Application problems test transfer, not reading complexity.
- Mastery requires evidence over time.
- Rewards encourage practice and persistence without replacing intrinsic understanding.

## Beta review process

1. Internal playtest with Reacher and family.
2. Review by an elementary math educator.
3. Small parent-managed beta with a diverse range of learners.
4. Analyze confusion points and strategy effectiveness without collecting unnecessary personal data.
5. Revise progression and repeat before public release.
